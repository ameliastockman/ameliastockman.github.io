PROJECT ROLL CALL

How can we improve the accessibility and inclusivity of higher education for disabled students — specifically within Design courses at Monash University?


When we study to become designers we are learning to create intentional change — purposeful, collective action directed towards a desired ideal — made with respect for our impact on the complex systems in which we exist.


Design is an act of world building, whether on the smallest or most expansive scale. For those who engage with it, an education in design can provide the powerful tools necessary to re-imagine and bring into reality a different life for ourselves and for the communities we belong to.


With the potential that this type of education can bring, it remains a pressing concern that access to such an opportunity is not equal for all. People with a disability still remain less likely than their non-disabled peers to possess a university qualificaition despite improvements made over the last decade.


Becoming a designer can provide a means for people with disabilities to have a direct ongoing voice in the development of the world around them. All minority groups experience the difficulties of living in a world that has not been built with people like them in mind — whether they were intentionally excluded and discriminated against, or unintentionally forgotten by designers who are limited to working with their own personal experiences and understanding as a foundation for their decisions.

A world that is truly inclusive and accessible can only be brought into reality by a diverse group of designers. And fostering a diverse group of designers begins with inclusive and accessible education.

UNDERSTANDING DISABILITY

18% of Australians have a disability, about 4.4 million or 1 in 6 people, and a further 22% have a long-term health condition but are not currently disabled. People with a disability make up the worlds largest minority, and are the only minority group which anyone can become a member of during their lifetime.


The United Nations Convention on the Rights of Persons with Disabilities supports the Social Model of Disability. This model defines disability as a result of the interaction between people living with physical, mental, intellectual or sensory impairments, and an environment filled with physical, attitudinal, communication and social barriers.


This perspective on disability infers that it is society that disables people and that it is society, not the individual, that must change to correct this.

Impairment is an expected and inherent part of human diversity, but disability — a persons inability to participate equally in society — is not an essential consequence of difference.

DISABILITY & HIGHER EDUCATION

People with disabilities continue to face considerable issues in regards to higher education access. Only 17% of people with a disability over the age of 20 have a bachelors degree or higher, compared to 35% of non-disabled Australians.


This disparity is not caused by lack of need or want. Numerous studies have documented disabled people's explicitly expressed desire for opportunities to pursue further education as a means of gaining meaningful employment, increased community participation, and a sense of purpose and identity.


Beyond the initial barriers to enrolment, disabled students remain at a disadvantage to their peers during their time at university. Students with a disability are less likely to positively rate their educational experience, are more likely to consider early departure from their course, and experience higher levels of social isolation which is cited as a significant reason students with a disability discontinue their studies.


In Australia, students with a disability face an average non-completion risk of about 36%, five percentage points higher than students without a disability. They are also more likely to be Indigenous, have a lower ATAR, be an older student, suffer financial stress, and be studying part time, all of which are factors that further increase the risk of non-completion.

DISABILITY AT MONASH UNIVERSITY

In 2019, Monash University had 44,651 domestic students enrolled, with 3,436 (or 7.7%) identifying as having a disability and disclosing this to the university. This is an improvement from only 3.5% in 2010 and 5.4% in 2015, but the disabled community still remains underrepresented within the university population.


Applying Monash University's expected retention rate to their 2019 domestic student population would suggest that 4,684 students did not continue their studies the following year, and 477 of those students identified as having a disability.


When considering the broader lives of those 477 disabled students, if they do not return in the future, they face the prospect of significant social and economic disadvantage. Having a higher level of education generally results in better employment outcomes and higher income, both of which are key factors in economic security and independence, as well as overall health and wellbeing prospects.

THE CONSEQUENCES OF INACCESSIBLITY

In Australia, only 48% of people aged 15-64 with a disability are employed compared to 80% of those without. Close to 40% of people living below the poverty line have a disability, but University graduates with a disability have similar full-time median salaries to university graduates without. These statistics highlight the importance of accessible education and proper supports in order to ensure students with disabilities can successfully complete their studies and enter professional practice.


Social isolation and loneliness is also significantly more prevalent amongst those with a disability. Universities serve as an important piece of social infrastructure by providing an environment that fosters interaction and social connectedness for students. For people with a disability, this regular and repeated contact can serve as one of their only opportunities to build relationships beyond their immediate circle.

ACCOMODATIONS AS DISABILITY SUPPORT

At Monash University, accomodations are provided by Student Support Services and available through registration with Disability Support Services.


But the process of requesting and receiving support and accomodations is a barrier itself. One survey of Australian university students found that 11% of respondents who identified as having a disability had not disclosed this to their university.


The requirement of disclosure in order to receive accomodations leaves students with disabilities exposed to discrimination and bias from staff and other students. Students reported belittling and negative comments about gaining an unfair advantage due to their accomodations, questioning of the validity of their disability and it’s impact, and a change in others perception of their intelligence and capability due to disclosure. 1 in 32 (3.1%) of those who have experienced disability discrimination in the last year have experienced it from a teacher or lecturer.


Accomodations by request require a student to know about their eligibility, understand what accomodations are available, and how disclosure could benefit them. Making these requests also requires students to push through their own feelings of shame, embarrassment, unworthiness, and the re-experiencing of trauma in order to receive much needed help.


Additionally, applying and managing access to accomodations involves a significant commitment of time and energy on the part of students who are already struggling, as well as that of teaching staff.

DESIGN FOR ACCESSIBILITY

Accessibility is the ongoing development of barrier free, flexible systems and environments that value difference and diversity, and allow for the inclusion of all people. Genuine inclusion means an opportunity for access, full participation, and success.


Courses should be designed to be as accessible as possible in order to minimise adjustments required to meet individual students needs. Without accessibility, barriers will continue to limit current and future student’s equitable access to higher education.


Universal Design offers a framework of seven principles for designing accessible systems, which can be applied to higher education design:



1. EQUITABLE USE

The design provides all users with the same opportunities, access, and outcomes without the need for modification or adaptation — it is identical where possible to achieve without harm, and equivalent when not. The design does not segregate or stigmatise any students.



2. FLEXIBLE MATERIALS, INSTRUCTION & ASSESSMENT OUTCOMES

The design takes into consideration diverse preferences and abilities, providing choice, flexibility, and adaptability. Materials can always be accessed in a variety of formats and beyond initial presentation in class hours. Care is taken to allow for the varied learning pace of students. Students are able to demonstrate knowledge through varied outcomes of their choice.



3. SIMPLE & INTUITIVE USE, WITH PREDICTABLE STRUCTURE & INSTRUCTION

The design eliminates unnecessary complexity and is presented clearly and consistently, with timely and constructive feedback.



4. PERCEPTIBLE, CLEAR COMMUNICATION OF INFORMATION

The design presents information and instruction in multiple ways — aurally, written, and visual, available digitally and printed — and is compatible with assistive technologies.



5. TOLERANCE FOR MISTAKES

Gaps and variance in skill and ability is anticipated, and instruction is designed to allow for this. Opportunities are offered for the application of improved knowledge after feedback. Students are encouraged to ask for help.



6. ELIMINATE PHYSICAL EFFORT

The design does not require unnecessary physical effort. The design takes into account the energy required to participate, and is mindful that energy capacity can vary substantially from student to student, and from day to day.



7. ENVIRONMENTAL ACCESSIBILITY

The design of the physical and digital environment is accessible to students with varied physical characteristics, communication needs, and social abilities. The environment is able to cater for assistive technology. It is also safe and free from discrimination.

ACCESSIBILITY AT MONASH UNIVERSITY

As it stands, Monash University still relies heavily on accomodations for supporting disabled students, and the accessibility practices defined in university guidelines are not consistently or wholly applied.


The Design Faculty is quite successful in utilising universal design principles in areas such as instruction and availability of materials. Students are supplied with course reading and presentation materials ahead of time, recorded lectures are uploaded online, and structured assignment briefs which clearly detail weekly expectations and assignment outcomes. But they still fall short in other basic accessibility areas, such as ensuring closed captioning or transcripts are available to all students for any video and audio content used.


The quick and necessary transition to remote learning as a result of the COVID-19 pandemic revealed the many benefits of online attendance. For students with a disability who have struggled to navigate the physical environment, or who are frequently absent from class due to illness, this alternative access radically altered the options available to them.


Known as The Curb Cut Effect, accessible design not only benefits people who are unable to access spaces without it, but it improves the experiences of everyone involved. For example, the option of remote access benefits some students with a disability, but the benefits extend to any student who has to travel a considerable distance to campus, students who are busy with work and family commitments, and students experiencing temporary illness.


With the wide variety of easy (and even free) technology we have available in today’s world, there is very little that is not a ‘reasonable adjustment’, and there are no adequate excuses for not implementing accessible design.

OUR RESPONSIBILITY AS DESIGN STUDENTS AND EDUCATORS

Designing for an accessible future requires reflecting on past and current choices. It is uncomfortable to acknowledge the ways in which we have failed to best support and include people, especially when our intentions have been good. Being a designer means recognising the power we have and the responsibility that power requires of us. We cannot afford to allow guilt to hold us back as we seek better understanding. A more inclusive future can begin today.


Just as there is no one student the same as another, there is no singular correct future of education or way to get there. It will always be ongoing, and such, it is important to focus on the process rather than an end vision. Designing for accessibility requires embracing complexity and working with the interdependence present in all systems, looking beyond just the immediate effects.


We must practice the futures we want to see because what we do at the small scale sets the patterns for the whole system. We cannot design a better world without putting into practice during the design process the principles and values we are hoping the outcome will embody. How we design matters as much as what we design, as does how we teach and learn to design.


People with disabilities have unique and valuable perspectives and skills as a result of their disability, and the ways in which they have had to adapt to overcome the barriers that have been placed in front of them. Disabled people engage in design thinking everyday. There exists an undervalued cohort of people who have the potential to become exceptional professional designers. We must look beyond the expected cohort; who have we assumed isn’t interested or capable of becoming designers, and are those assumptions correct?


A commitment needs to be made to not only ensuring the environment is accessible, but that people with disabilities are actively sought out, invited and encouraged to undertake design studies at Monash. A ramp is not welcome enough. Once at Monash, people with disabilities must be given the opportunity to be involved in the decision making process for university policies and course design, especially those which directly concern them.


How can the academics of the Monash Design Faculty lead the creation of a design culture that invites in and empowers disabled designers?

LET MADA KNOW:

WHAT CAN WE DO AS INDIVIDUALS?

It can be tempting to wait for institutionally directed or enforced action before making the changes necessary for accessibility, but there are smaller individual choices we can be making to move us towards an inclusive future today. Consider what you can be doing to contribute to accessibility, both at university and elsewhere.


As a student, are you engaged with learning from the disabled community on the access issues and discrimination they face? Can you apply best practices for accessibility to your own designs and assignments? Are your presentations accessible to other students in your class? Do you designs use adequate colour contrast and font size for maximum readability? If you are creating or showing a video, can you provide closed captioning or a transcript?


As an educator, what have you done to make your classroom accessible? Are you informed of and putting into practice the universities accessibility policies? Do you know what support is available to students, and are you passing that knowledge? Do you initiate an open dialogue at the beginning of the semester with your students about what they need in class and for assessments in order to learn best? Are you recognising the unique perspectives of students with a disability and the valuing their knowledge and experience as an asset for class learning? Are you working to unlearn your own biases and ableism?

No one else is going to do this for us, it's time to make ourselves heard. There is so much we can learn from each other. What you have to say about your experiences and your ideas about how to improve the accessibility of your education is important.
